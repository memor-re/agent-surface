# Negotiation Lenses

Use this reference when the situation is underspecified or the user is reacting to a quote, demand, or blocker without a clean structure.

## Minimum Inputs

Collect or infer:

- objective
- current ask or quote
- required outcomes
- nice-to-have outcomes
- non-negotiables
- deadline or timing pressure
- approvals needed
- known risks
- alternatives

If more than two of these are unknown, say so and keep the first recommendation provisional.

## Situation Read

Clarify:

- what the user is trying to secure
- what the counterparty is trying to secure
- what each side is trying to avoid
- what is already agreed
- what remains undefined

Do not let a shorthand ask hide the real scope. A simple "deal" often contains scope, timing, review burden, rights, approval steps, risk transfer, and future obligations.

## Leverage Scan

Check:

- alternatives or BATNA
- urgency asymmetry
- reputational value
- future relationship value
- execution complexity
- approval bottlenecks
- switching cost
- information advantage
- time-to-close advantage

Leverage is not only money. It can come from speed, clarity, trust, repeat business, narrower scope, risk reduction, or certainty of execution.

## Tradeable Variables

Typical levers:

- price or fee
- scope or deliverables
- rights or permissions
- duration
- timeline
- exclusivity or restrictions
- review burden
- support obligations
- payment terms
- renewal or extension options
- reporting or documentation obligations

If the negotiation is stuck on one variable, move laterally to another.

## Thresholds

Define:

- opening anchor: the first counter position
- target: the good outcome the user wants
- walk-away: the maximum acceptable cost or minimum acceptable structure

If the user has not set thresholds, help them define them before drafting a forceful response.

## Escalation Triggers

Escalate when:

- the user lacks approval authority
- the counterparty asks for broad undefined rights or obligations
- financial exposure materially changes
- reputation, privacy, trust, or compliance risk increases
- the counterparty rejects key protections
- the negotiation depends on facts the user cannot verify
